🛠️ CAM & TOOLPATH GENERATION

Creating 2D Toolpaths, Setup Sheets, & G-Code

From 3D Model to CNC Instructions

You've designed your robot parts and configured your machines. Now it's time to bridge the gap between CAD and physical cutting. In this lesson, you'll learn how to generate precise toolpaths that tell the CNC machine exactly how to manufacture your parts.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the difference between geometry and toolpaths
  • Create 2D contour and pocket toolpaths in Fusion 360
  • Configure setup sheets and work coordinate systems
  • Set cutting parameters (feeds, speeds, depths)
  • Simulate toolpaths to verify before cutting
  • Generate G-code using post-processors

Prerequisites: Completed Lesson 2 (Machine & Tool Library setup). You should have both the OMIO and Crossfire machines configured in Fusion 360.

📐 WORK COORDINATE SYSTEM (WCS)

What is a Setup?

A Setup defines the relationship between your part, the stock material, and the machine. It establishes where X0, Y0, Z0 are located—the "origin" from which all tool movements are calculated.

Common Zero Positions for Robot Parts

BOTTOM-LEFT CORNER

X0, Y0: Lower-left corner of stock

Z0: Top of stock material

Use Case: Best for rectangular parts with clear edges (robot decks)

CENTER OF PART

X0, Y0: Geometric center

Z0: Top of stock

Use Case: Symmetrical parts (circular plates, gears)

⚠️ Critical: Your physical zero position on the CNC machine MUST match the zero position you define in Fusion 360. Misalignment = crashed tool or ruined part.

📦 DEFINING STOCK MATERIAL

Stock vs. Model

  • Model: Your final part design (the geometry you created in the Design workspace)
  • Stock: The raw material you'll cut from (larger than the finished part)

Stock Setup Example: Robot Deck

Model Dimensions:     200mm × 150mm × 3mm (finished part)
Stock Dimensions:     220mm × 170mm × 3.2mm (raw polycarbonate)

Extra Stock:
  • X/Y sides: +10mm each side (clamping area)
  • Z top: +0.2mm (surfacing allowance)
  • Z bottom: 0mm (bottom sits on spoilboard)

Creating the Setup

  • 1 Manufacture workspace → Setup → New Setup
  • 2 Select OMIO X8-2200 machine
  • 3 Set Work Coordinate System to bottom-left corner
  • 4 Define Stock → Relative Size Box
  • 5 Offset: X+10, Y+10, Z+0.2mm (sides and top)

✂️ 2D CONTOUR TOOLPATH

What is a Contour?

A 2D Contour follows the outline of your part, cutting around the perimeter. This is typically the final operation that separates your part from the stock material.

Contour Parameters

GEOMETRY

  • Select edges to cut
  • Choose inside/outside/on-line
  • Set cut direction (climb/conventional)

HEIGHTS

  • Clearance: 10mm (safe travel height)
  • Retract: 5mm (between cuts)
  • Top: 0mm (stock top)
  • Bottom: -3.2mm (cut through)

Creating a Contour

  • 1 2D → 2D Contour
  • 2 Select 6.35mm flat end mill
  • 3 Select part outline (edges)
  • 4 Set Side to "Outside" (cut around part)
  • 5 Add tabs (small connections to hold part in place)
  • 6 Set bottom height to -3.3mm (0.1mm deeper than stock)

🕳️ 2D POCKET TOOLPATH

What is a Pocket?

A 2D Pocket removes material from an enclosed area—like holes, recesses, or weight-reduction cutouts in your robot deck.

Pocket vs. Contour

2D CONTOUR

Cuts around a profile (outline)

Follows edges

Used for: Part separation, slots

2D POCKET

Removes interior material

Fills bounded area with toolpath

Used for: Holes, recesses, lightening

Pocket Strategies

OFFSET STRATEGY:
  • Spirals inward from boundary
  • Smooth, continuous motion
  • Best for most pockets

ZIGZAG STRATEGY:
  • Back-and-forth passes
  • Faster but rougher
  • Use for large areas with finishing pass

Creating a Pocket for Mounting Holes

  • 1 2D → 2D Pocket
  • 2 Select hole boundaries (sketch circles or model pockets)
  • 3 Set Bottom Height to recess depth (e.g., -2mm)
  • 4 Choose Offset clearing strategy
  • 5 Set Stepover to 50% of tool diameter (3.175mm for 6.35mm tool)

⚡ FEEDS, SPEEDS & DEPTH OF CUT

The Three Critical Parameters

SPINDLE SPEED (RPM)

How fast the tool spins

Polycarbonate: 18,000 RPM

Plywood: 18,000 RPM

Aluminum: 12,000 RPM

FEED RATE (mm/min)

How fast the tool moves through material

Polycarbonate: 1500 mm/min

Plywood: 2000 mm/min

Aluminum: 800 mm/min

DEPTH OF CUT (DOC)

How deep each pass goes

Polycarbonate: 1.5mm per pass

Plywood: 3mm per pass

Aluminum: 0.5mm per pass

⚠️ Conservative Settings for Beginners: Start at 75% of recommended speeds. You can always go faster, but you can't un-break a tool!

Rule of Thumb: Listen to the Cut

  • Smooth humming: Perfect! ✅
  • Squealing/chattering: Feed rate too slow or RPM too high ⚠️
  • Grinding/laboring: Feed rate too fast or RPM too low ⚠️

🎬 SIMULATING TOOLPATHS

Why Simulate?

Simulation lets you preview the entire cutting process before you waste material or break tools. It's like a dress rehearsal for your CNC job.

What to Look For During Simulation

  • 1 Tool Collisions: Does the tool hit clamps or fixtures?
  • 2 Material Removal: Is all intended material removed?
  • 3 Excess Material: Any "islands" left behind?
  • 4 Part Dimensions: Does final part match design?
  • 5 Rapid Movements: Does tool retract to safe heights?

Running a Simulation

  • 1 Click Actions → Simulate
  • 2 Click Play to watch full simulation
  • 3 Use Slider to step through toolpath slowly
  • 4 Toggle Stock visibility to see part emerge
  • 5 Check Compare to see design vs. simulated result

Pro Tip: Run simulation at 2-4x speed to quickly check for major errors, then slow down to 0.5x to inspect critical areas like corners and pockets.

📎 TABS: KEEPING PARTS SECURE

The Tab Problem

When you cut completely through your stock, what holds the part in place? Without tabs, your part becomes a projectile as soon as the final cut completes.

What are Tabs?

Tabs are small "bridges" of material intentionally left uncut during the toolpath. They keep the part attached to the stock until you manually break them off.

Tab Configuration

Recommended Tab Settings:

  Tab Height:     1.5mm (half your stock thickness)
  Tab Width:      10mm
  Number of Tabs: 3-6 (depending on part size)

  Placement:
    • Distribute evenly around perimeter
    • Avoid placing on critical edges
    • Position on flat sections (not corners)

Removing Tabs After Cutting

  • Use flush-cut pliers or small saw to cut tabs
  • Sand tab remnants flush with part edge
  • File or deburr sharp edges

⚠️ Safety Note: NEVER attempt to cut tabs while part is still on the machine. Remove workpiece first, then break tabs at the bench.

💾 GENERATING G-CODE

What is G-Code?

G-code is the language CNC machines speak. It's a text file containing line-by-line instructions: "move to X100 Y50", "spin tool at 18000 RPM", "cut to Z-3mm", etc.

Sample G-Code

G21          ; Set units to millimeters
G90          ; Absolute positioning
M3 S18000    ; Spindle on, 18,000 RPM
G0 Z10       ; Rapid move to safe height
G0 X50 Y50   ; Rapid move to start position
G1 Z-1.5 F500  ; Plunge to depth, 500 mm/min
G1 X150 F1500  ; Cut to X150, 1500 mm/min
G1 Y100      ; Cut to Y100
G0 Z10       ; Retract to safe height
M5           ; Spindle off
M2           ; Program end

Post-Processing Steps

  • 1 Right-click Setup → Post Process
  • 2 Select omio-grbl post-processor
  • 3 Click Post to generate .nc file
  • 4 Save to USB drive or network folder
  • 5 Load file into CNC controller (UGS, Carbide Motion, etc.)

✅ LESSON SUMMARY

Key Takeaways

  • CAM translates 3D geometry into machine instructions (toolpaths)
  • Setup defines work coordinate system and stock dimensions
  • 2D Contour cuts outlines; 2D Pocket removes interior material
  • Feeds, speeds, and depth of cut determine cutting efficiency and quality
  • Simulation catches errors before they become expensive mistakes
  • Post-processors generate machine-specific G-code from toolpaths

The CAM Mindset

Good CAM work is about process planning—thinking through the sequence of operations, selecting the right tools, and balancing speed with quality. Your first job will take hours. Your tenth will take minutes.
