Applied Engineering & Robotics**
Week 1 Overview (August 25--29, 2025)
Focus: Introduce students to the Engineering Design Process (EDP)
and build teamwork through prototyping and testing exercises.
Objectives: Break the ice, establish safety and respect rules,
introduce EDP steps, and practice rapid prototyping/testing for
engagement and skill-building (Syllabus Objective 1; Excel Standards
IX.A.1--2, XI.A.2).
Google Classroom Stream:
Homework:
Day 1 (August 25, 2025, 22 min)
Topic: Introductions
Objective: Break ice, get familiar with each other (Syllabus
Objective 10; Excel II.B.1--3).
Media: Intro Slides
Prompt: Name and one thing you have done that was extremely
adventurous, or an adventure you would like to do.
Assignment: Day 1 Questionnaire
Notes: Keep it light and engaging to build rapport.
Day 2 (August 26, 2025)
Topic: Lab & Shop Tour, Safety Brief, Intro to EDP
Objective: Understand safety rules (PPE, conduct), respect rules (no
tampering with others' projects), and explore robotic solutions
(Syllabus Objectives 1, 6; Excel IX.A.1, Vicksburg Lab Rules 1--3).
Media: Syllabus, Shop/Lab Rules
Assignment: Syllabus and Rules, returned by end of Labor Day
weekend.
Notes: Use previous year's robots to spark interest. Emphasize
safety protocols.
Day 3 (August 27, 2025)
Topic: Introduction to the Engineering Design Process, Group
Assignments, Paper Glider Challenge
Objective: Reinforce EDP (Ask, Research & Imagine, Create/Test),
promote teamwork, and practice rapid prototyping/testing (Syllabus
Objectives 1, 10; Excel IX.A.1--2, XI.A.2).
Media: Unit 1.1 Slides, Paper Glider Challenge Slides, Music
Assignment: Journal Entry, due by noon the next day.
Notes: Use tape labeled with team numbers on the floor for testing.
Clarify challenge is for paper airplanes (one sheet per plane, not
wadded paper).
Day 4 (August 28, 2025)
Topic: Spaghetti Tower Challenge
Objective: Reinforce EDP (Ask, Research & Imagine, Create/Test),
teamwork (one entry per team), and rapid prototyping/testing (Syllabus
Objectives 1, 10; Excel IX.A.1--2, XI.A.2).
Media: Spaghetti Tower Slide, Music
Assignment: Journal Entry, due by noon the next day.
Notes: Use tape on cutting mats for equal string measurement.
Replace up to 5 broken spaghetti pieces upon return of broken pieces.
Warn teams to test early to avoid last-minute failures.
Week 2 Overview (September 2--5, 2025)
Focus: Build a design brief for a desktop cell phone stand, focusing
on the Ask, Research, and Imagine steps of the EDP, and introduce the
Pugh Matrix for evaluating ideas.
Objectives: Develop skills in defining problems, researching
solutions, brainstorming designs, and evaluating options while
incorporating ethical considerations. Practice paper/pencil sketching
for visualization (Syllabus Objectives 1, 12; Excel IX.A.1--2,
XI.A.2--3, I.A.3).
Google Classroom Stream:
Homework:
Daily: Journal entries (1--2 paragraphs, due next day by noon):
Day 5 (September 2, 2025)
Topic: EDP Step 1 -- Ask & Design Brief Introduction
Objective: Define the problem for a desktop cell phone stand and
draft the problem statement section of the design brief, incorporating
ethical considerations (Syllabus Objectives 1, 12; Excel IX.A.1, I.A.3).
Media: Design Brief Intro Slides
Assignment: Journal Entry, due by noon the next day.
Notes: Introduce design brief components (problem statement,
research summary, brainstormed ideas). Teams define the problem (e.g.,
securely holding a smartphone with adjustable angles for desk use). Ask
questions of the "customer" (instructor) to clarify needs (e.g.,
stability, portability). Sketch initial concepts on paper (e.g., stand
base shape). Emphasize constraints (e.g., materials like PLA or wood,
budget under \$10, buildable in 2 weeks) and ethics (e.g., sustainable
materials).
Day 6 (September 3, 2025)
Topic: EDP Step 2 -- Research & Design Brief Update
Objective: Research existing cell phone stand designs and materials,
updating the research summary section of the design brief (Syllabus
Objectives 1, 12; Excel IX.A.3, I.C.2).
Media: Research Skills Slides
Assignment: Journal Entry, due by noon the next day.
Notes: Students use Chromebooks to research phone stand designs
(e.g., Instructables, DIY sites) and materials (e.g., 3D-printed PLA,
wood, recycled plastics). Document 3--5 sources in Google Docs, citing
properly (Lab Rules Section 9). Update design brief with research
summary. Include paper sketches of researched designs. Discuss how
research refines problem definition (e.g., angle adjustment mechanisms).
Day 7 (September 4, 2025)
Topic: EDP Step 3 -- Imagine & Design Brief Update
Objective: Brainstorm solutions for the cell phone stand and update
the brainstormed ideas section of the design brief with paper sketches
(Syllabus Objectives 1, 10; Excel XI.A.2, II.A.1).
Media: Brainstorming Slides
Assignment: Journal Entry, due by noon the next day.
Notes: Teams brainstorm 5+ ideas (e.g., foldable stands, weighted
bases, adjustable hinges) using mind mapping. Create detailed
paper/pencil sketches for top ideas. Update design brief with ideas and
sketches (photos uploaded to Google Docs). Discuss feasibility and
ethics (e.g., recyclable materials, minimal waste). The instructor
provides "customer" feedback.
Day 8 (September 5, 2025)
Topic: Pugh Matrix & Design Brief Draft Finalization
Objective: Evaluate brainstormed ideas using the Pugh Matrix and
finalize design brief drafts (Syllabus Objectives 1, 12; Excel XI.A.3,
IX.A.4).
Media: Pugh Matrix Slides
Assignment: Finalize design brief, due Monday, September 8, noon.
Notes: Introduce Pugh Matrix (criteria: stability, cost, ease of
use, ethics/sustainability). Teams evaluate their brainstormed ideas,
selecting the top solution using a provided template (Google Doc or
paper). Finalize draft brief (problem statement, research summary, ideas
with sketches, Pugh Matrix). Peer review for clarity. Instructor as
"customer" provides feedback.

Post-Semester Reflections (December 13, 2025)
Current Status
As of December 13, approximately half of the class has completed the build phase of their robot. Student engagement has been inconsistent, with significant challenges in maintaining focus and accountability throughout the robot project.
Major Challenges Identified
1. Robot Part Iteration Log Implementation
What Was Intended:
Students make a part, test it, record results, iterate with improvements, and test again
Each group (3-4 students) responsible for ~8 components
Documentation of the iterative design process as a major grade component
What Actually Happened:
Many students failed to document their iterations despite producing multiple versions of parts
After 5 weeks of extending the deadline, some students submitted nothing at all
Students are now receiving failing grades for this major assignment component
The iterative testing and documentation process largely did not materialize as planned
Impact:
Loss of learning opportunity around the engineering design process
Major grade component failures affecting student outcomes
Undermines the pedagogical value of the entire robot project
2. CAD Quality and Workflow Issues
Observed Problems:
Extremely complex Fusion 360 timelines with multiple sketches, extrusions, and unconstrained holes
In many cases, parts that should require only one sketch and one extrusion have become unnecessarily complicated
Difficult to help students troubleshoot their CAD drawings due to poor organization
Students lack discipline in proper CAD practices
Root Causes (Suspected):
Kevin Kennedy YouTube tutorial series may be teaching suboptimal habits
While tutorials explain returning to underlying sketches to change dimensions, they also show adding more features and sketches after the first sketch/extrusion
Students primarily adopt the habit of adding more sketches rather than planning properly upfront
Lack of reinforcement of good CAD hygiene during tutorial phase
3. Group Dynamics Challenges
Range of Experiences:
Some groups worked collaboratively and effectively
Other groups had one student doing most/all of the work
Worst case: In one group, two students did everything while a third spent four weeks pretending to work on protoboard standoffs while actually playing internet games
Lack of strict oversight allowed disengaged students to avoid accountability
Contributing Factors:
Groups of 3-4 students provide hiding places for slackers
Insufficient monitoring systems to catch non-participation early
Need for better accountability structures within group work
Potential Improvements for Next Year
CAD Instruction Revision - Component-Based Curriculum
Current Approach Issues:
Kevin Kennedy's "Fusion 360 in 30 Days" series is professional, free, short, and comprehensive
However, it may be teaching bad habits that undermine CAD quality
Generic exercises (dog bowls, doorstops, phone cases) disconnect from actual project needs
Students complete tutorials then start over learning robot components
NEW PROPOSED APPROACH - Common Component Design:
Fundamental Change:
Instead of each group designing their own unique robot parts, the entire class will work on common components together. These components become the CAD lessons themselves.
Component-Based CAD Lessons:
Replace generic tutorials with actual robot components:
1. Motor Mounts - Teach basic extrusion, holes, constraints
2. IR Sensor Mounts - Teach clearances, positioning (15mm ground clearance requirement)
3. Battery Holders - Teach relief features for wire management
4. Robot Deck - Teach hole patterns, wire pass-throughs, mounting considerations
5. Electronics Mounts - Teach standoffs, component spacing, accessibility
Integrated Learning Approach:
Each component lesson includes:
CAD Techniques: The specific Fusion 360 skills needed for that component
Functional Understanding: How the component works in the robot system
Design Requirements: Special considerations specific to that component
IR sensor needs 15mm clearance from ground
Battery holder needs relief for wires
Deck needs holes for wire pass-through
Motor mounts need precise shaft alignment
Electronics need proper standoff heights
Implementation Strategy:
1. Use select Kennedy videos only for foundational tool demonstrations
2. Explicitly teach CAD hygiene before each component:
Plan the sketch completely before creating it
Fully constrain all sketches
Use single sketch + single extrusion when possible
Minimize timeline complexity
3. Build each component as a class with instructor-led demonstration
4. Students follow along, creating the same component with guidance
5. Component variations can be assigned for differentiation
Benefits of This Approach:
Eliminates disconnect between tutorial exercises and project application
Accelerates timeline - no need to "start over" after tutorials
Contextualizes learning - students understand WHY design decisions matter
Reinforces good CAD practices - instructor can model and enforce proper workflow
Provides common baseline - all students have working components to build with
Enables better troubleshooting - instructor knows exactly what timeline should look like
Teaches systems thinking - students learn how components interact
Reduces variability - fewer unique designs means fewer failure points during build
Maintains creativity - students still customize and improve base designs
Common Components Eliminate:
Groups working in isolation on incompatible designs
Students creating overly complex or non-functional parts
Instructor inability to provide targeted help due to unique designs
Time spent debugging individual student design issues
Build phase delays from failed custom components
Iteration Documentation System
Considerations:
Current worksheet-based system failed to capture actual work
Need more integrated documentation that happens during work, not after
Consider alternative approaches:
Digital portfolios with photo requirements at each stage
Version control in Fusion 360 with mandatory naming conventions
Checkpoint reviews where progress must be demonstrated
Integration with 3D printing queue (must document before printing)
Group Accountability
Possible Solutions:
Smaller groups (pairs) reduce hiding places
Individual component ownership within teams
Regular individual check-ins and progress demonstrations
Peer evaluation components
Earlier intervention when non-participation is observed
Shop Tool Safety Training - Timing Issue
Problem Identified:
During the robot build phase, students are using multiple shop tools that were not covered during the initial safety unit:
Drill press
Pedestal grinder
Hand grinder
Hand drill
Current Gap:
Unit 4 (Safety, Week 9 in October) covers OSHA, LOTO, PPE, and SDS interpretation
Specific tool operation and safety practices are not included
Students encounter these tools during build phase without prior formal training
"Just-in-time" training during build is rushed and inconsistent
Proposed Solution:
Expand Unit 4 (Safety & Robotics Foundations) to include hands-on tool training modules:
Additional Safety Lessons:
1. Drill Press Safety & Operation
Setup, workpiece securing, speed selection
Proper bit installation and removal
Chuck key discipline (never leave in chuck)
Safe operation procedures
2. Pedestal Grinder Safety & Operation
Tool rest adjustment (1/8" max gap)
Proper grinding techniques
Eye protection requirements
Wheel integrity checks
3. Hand Power Tools (Grinder & Drill)
Secure workpiece requirements
Proper grip and stance
Bit/disc changes and securing
Electrical safety considerations
4. Tool Certification System
Demonstration of competency before independent use
Signed certification cards for each tool
Progressive access based on demonstrated skills
Similar to CNC router license system (Week 11)
Implementation in Unit 4:
Extend safety unit by 2-3 days to accommodate tool training
Students must demonstrate competency on each tool
Safety quiz includes tool-specific questions
Tool certifications required before build phase begins
This front-loads training so build phase can proceed safely and efficiently
Benefits:
Students properly trained before need arises
Consistent safety instruction for all students
Reduces build phase interruptions for training
Creates accountability through certification system
Parallels real-world shop certification requirements
