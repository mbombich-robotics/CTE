A Message from the Control Freaks
April 2026

Dear Friend and Supporter of the Control Freaks,

Every spring, when the competition season wraps up and the robot gets put away, I find myself wanting to reach out to the people who made it all possible. This year especially, I wanted to make sure you heard directly from us — because this season was something worth sharing.

The short version: Team 8126 qualified for the FIRST in Michigan District Championship and came home with two awards — the Team Sustainability Award at our second district event, and the Innovation in Controls Award at the state competition. Winning a judged award at the state level, competing against Michigan's top programs, is a milestone this team has been working toward for years. Your support is a part of why we got there.

What Your Investment Made Possible This Season
  • A competition robot built from scratch over a twelve-week season — six weeks of design and build, six weeks of district competition. Students designed four integrated subsystems: a collector, conveyor, indexer, and flywheel shooter, along with a custom multi-camera vision system built in-house on a Raspberry Pi.
  • Two major awards, including recognition at the state level for the team's controls system — a reflection of the depth of technical work students put in far beyond just making the robot move.
  • A pipeline that's proving itself. Seven students on this year's roster started in our FIRST LEGO League program in the fourth grade — one led all of our awards submissions, coached the team through judging, and ran our competition scouting operation. That kind of development doesn't happen by accident.
Three Shooter Paths — Visually and Mechanically Identical

The Control Freaks program has been building engineers in Vicksburg for years, and its growth ultimately led to the creation of a dedicated Applied Engineering and Robotics class at Vicksburg High School — formalizing what the team had been doing all along. Students in that class use the same tools, workflows, and design processes that professionals use: CAD software, CNC machining, 3D printing, and structured engineering documentation. The competitive season is woven directly into the curriculum as a real-world engineering project with real stakes.

The program operates a team facility equipped with CNC machining, ten 3D printers, and a practice field built at two-thirds regulation size — giving students professional-grade resources for prototyping and driver training.

Looking ahead, we are already planning for the 2027 season. We have a growing roster, a program that's earning recognition at the state level, and students who are more capable every year. What we need most is the continued partnership of supporters like you — people and organizations who believe that investing in young engineers is worth it.

Thank you for being part of what we are building here. Your name is on this season just as much as ours is. We are proud to represent our community, and we hope to keep doing it for years to come.

With gratitude,

Matt Bombich
