Introduction
Team Overview

Team 8126 — the Vicksburg Control Freaks — is an FRC team from Vicksburg, Michigan (pop. ~3,400). In six years we have grown from a program in an 80×25 ft portable classroom into a full mechatronics shop with a half-court practice field, programming room, and classroom. Our program is almost entirely self-funded and operates a complete FLL → FTC → FRC pipeline.

Design Philosophy

Our motto — "Put it together. Take it apart." — is the operating principle behind every design decision. We build to learn, iterate to improve, and discard what isn't working. This mindset produced a robot that, by its third iteration, is precise, modular, visually unified, and competition-hardened.

System Architecture

Four Arducam OV9281 cameras run PhotonVision with multi-tag PNP estimation. All four camera feeds are weighted and fused with drivetrain odometry via WPILib's SwerveDrivePoseEstimator and ADIS16470 IMU, giving the robot a continuously accurate field position.

Routine Structure

At match start the robot reads its position via AprilTag detection and selects one of six routines (two per starting position, both alliances). Each routine:

  • Navigate under the trough; collect game pieces
  • Return over the hump — saves ~2 sec vs. the long way
  • Settle; auto-aim using live AprilTags to reestablish pose
  • Score ~25 balls; repeat for a second cycle

Executed in competition twice at our most recent event. Both runs were identical — all marks hit, 95% of loaded game pieces scored. Average total: ~50 balls per autonomous period.

Design Process

Every subsystem followed: problem definition → requirements → concept generation → Pugh analysis → prototype → controlled testing → iteration.

Shooter: Fixed Hood vs. Articulating

Three concepts evaluated via weighted Pugh matrix (7 criteria). Fixed hood selected. ~3 hours of structured 10-shot trials established 68–67° as the optimal angle. Final iteration refined to 62°, 0.35" compression, enabling simultaneous collect-and-shoot.

Drivetrain: Belly Pan Iterations

Steel (rigid, too heavy) → hybrid steel/PC (improved) → full polycarbonate (final, ~29.3 lbs). Measurably better acceleration and driver control at each stage.

Climber: Strategic Removal

V4 climber (9.34 lbs) lacked torque and lateral stability. Two V5 paths engineered (magnetic lock, active stability). Strategic decision: remove the climber, reallocate 9.34 lbs to scoring. Software complete. Modular chassis footprint preserved for future re-integration.

Collector: Simplicity as a Design Goal

Multi-roller prototypes jammed under load. Final design: two-roller, single-motor belt system with Cat Tongue grip and 0.25–0.5" compression. Fewer failure points, better synchronization, more consistent acquisition.

Software Iteration Examples

  • Encoder wraparound at 0/1 boundary caused PID to command wrong direction — fixed via wraparound handling
  • Pre-spin cap too high → voltage drop blocked other inputs — calibrated to lower idle RPM
  • PathPlanner replaced Choreo for improved trajectory error diagnostics

Industrial Design Award
Recognizes a robot where form and function are unified — material choices, visual design, and structural decisions working as a coherent whole.

Clear Polycarbonate as a Design Language

All primary panels are clear polycarbonate — intentional on multiple levels. Aesthetically, it produces a sharp, modern industrial appearance. Functionally, drivers see payload status in real time without sensors or cameras. Pit crew diagnose issues at a glance without removing covers.

PC was also selected for impact resistance. Competition contact is anticipated and designed for — tolerance between collector and hopper absorbs impacts without transferring force to adjacent components.

Unified Visual System

Every shooter panel is flush-aligned with no visible offset. All three shooter paths are visually and mechanically identical. The assembly appears machined as a single part. This is the direct result of CNC-routed components and ±0.10 mm carbon fiber spacers.

The Shooter: A Quality Benchmark

Spacers cut from carbon fiber arrow shafts to ±0.10 mm hold wheel position in all three shooter paths. The result: panels are flush, wheels sit true, and the assembly appears machined as a single piece.

Three independent velocity loops eliminate follower interference. Pre-spun at calibrated idle RPM, shot-to-shot consistency is high and spin-up delay is eliminated.

Modularity as a Quality Practice

Removing the live bottom from the shooter assembly in V3 was a quality decision. Each module is independently inspectable and serviceable without disturbing adjacent systems. Belt tension on the live bottom improved as a side effect. The robot is faster to repair and reassembled more reliably after maintenance.

Opening Our Facility

Several teams have been formally invited to practice at our half-court field — full-scale space unavailable to most small programs in our region. These sessions are collaborative by design: our students and theirs share the floor, compare notes, and solve problems together.

Pit Row and Event Support

At every event we assist in pit row: sharing parts and materials, helping other teams with mechanical diagnosis, and offering programming support. Our team is consistently available to help with sensor integration, motor controller configuration, and autonomous debugging.

Recycling Initiative — Community Partnership

When transitioning into our new mechatronics shop, we partnered with the Vicksburg Rotary Club to recycle more than 3,833 lbs of scrap metal from the old shop. The proceeds — $560.83 — were added to the Rotary Club's scholarship fund for Vicksburg high school graduates.

This initiative demonstrates Gracious Professionalism extending beyond the competition floor: finding a way to serve the community in the process of serving our team.
