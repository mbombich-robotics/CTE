The Matthew Henne Incident

What Happened?

  • Young maintenance worker at amusement park
  • Entered restricted area of operating roller coaster
  • Attempting to retrieve lost item during operation
  • Struck by moving roller coaster car
  • Fatal accident that could have been prevented

Key Safety Failures

  • No physical barriers preventing access during operation
  • No sensors to detect human presence in danger zones
  • No automatic shutdown when unauthorized entry occurred
  • Inadequate training and supervision

Engineering Controls: Safety by Design

What are Engineering Controls?

Physical solutions designed into equipment and workspaces to reduce or eliminate hazards before human error can occur.

🚧

Barriers

Physical guards that block access

📡

Sensors

Detect dangerous conditions

🔒

Interlocks

Prevent operation when unsafe

Why Engineering Controls Matter

The Hierarchy of Controls

From most effective to least effective:

  • 1. Elimination: Remove the hazard entirely
  • 2. Substitution: Replace with something safer
  • 3. Engineering Controls: Isolate people from hazards
  • 4. Administrative Controls: Change work procedures
  • 5. PPE: Personal protective equipment (least effective alone)

Engineering controls are the 3rd most effective way to prevent accidents!

How Controls Could've Saved Matthew

Interlocked Doors

Automatically stop the ride if a restricted door is opened

Example: Elevator doors won't close if something is in the way

Motion Sensors

Detect when someone enters a danger zone and halt operation

Example: Automatic garage door sensors

Physical Barriers

Block access to moving parts during operation - make it physically impossible to enter

Example: Machine guards on table saws, milling machines

Engineering Controls in Action

SawStop Technology

Table saw with flesh-sensing technology

  • Detects electrical conductivity of human skin
  • Stops blade in milliseconds
  • Retracts blade below table
  • Prevents amputation injuries

CNC Machine Enclosures

Fully enclosed cutting area

  • Clear polycarbonate shields
  • Interlocked doors stop spindle
  • Contains chips and coolant
  • Impossible to reach cutting tool during operation

Applying Controls Here

Plasma CNC

  • Water table: Arrests sparks and reduces fire hazard
  • Ventilation: Removes harmful fumes
  • Emergency stop: Large red button accessible from all sides

Table Saw

  • Blade guards: Pre-installed to cover blade
  • Riving knife: Prevents kickback
  • SawStop technology: Detects skin contact
  • Push sticks: Keep hands away from blade

3D Printer Enclosures

  • Temperature isolation: Hot end can reach 500°F
  • Fume extraction: Removes plastic vapors
  • Clear viewing: Monitor without opening

Design Challenge: Part 1

Scenario: Robotic Arm Workstation

You're designing a workstation where a robotic arm picks and places parts. The arm moves quickly and has significant force. Operators need to load parts and remove finished products.

Hazards to Consider

  • Operators could reach into work area while arm is moving
  • Arm could strike operator causing injury
  • Parts could be ejected at high speed
  • Pinch points between arm and fixtures

Design Challenge: Part 2

Your Task

Work with your team to propose one engineering control that would make this workstation safer.

Consider:

  • How will operators safely load/unload parts?
  • What prevents someone from entering while the robot operates?
  • What happens if someone does enter the danger zone?
  • How can we make the system "fail-safe"?

Time: 10 minutes to sketch and discuss with your team

Possible Engineering Solutions

Light Curtain System

  • Invisible beam of light surrounds work area
  • Breaking beam immediately stops robot
  • Used in industrial automation
  • Allows access without physical barriers

Interlocked Safety Cage

  • Physical barrier around robot
  • Door with safety interlock
  • Robot stops when door opens
  • Cannot restart until door closed and reset button pressed

Collaborative Robot (Cobot)

  • Force-sensing technology in joints
  • Automatically stops if it contacts unexpected resistance
  • Limited speed and force by design
  • Safe for human-robot collaboration

The Swiss Cheese Model of Accident Causation

Why Multiple Layers of Protection Matter

No single safety measure is perfect. Each has "holes" or weaknesses. Accidents happen when holes align across all layers.

Engineering controls + Training + PPE + Procedures = Maximum Safety

Lessons for Our Lab

Key Takeaways from Matthew Henne Incident

  • Never bypass safety systems - they exist for a reason
  • Design systems to be fail-safe - assume humans will make mistakes
  • Multiple layers of protection - don't rely on just one control
  • If it seems unsafe, stop and ask - better to pause than proceed
  • Respect locked-out equipment - someone's life depends on it
