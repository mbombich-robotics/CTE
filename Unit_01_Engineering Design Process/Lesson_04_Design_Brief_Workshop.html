What is a Design Brief?

Definition

A design brief is a comprehensive document that guides an engineering project from concept to completion.

It Answers:

  • What problem are we solving?
  • Who are the stakeholders?
  • What are the requirements?
  • What are the constraints?
  • What solutions exist?
  • Which solution is best?

Why It Matters:

  • Keeps team aligned
  • Documents decisions
  • Guides development
  • Communicates with clients
  • Used in industry!

DAY 1 Problem Statement (Ask)

Purpose:

Define the problem clearly to start the EDP

What to Include:

  • Problem Description: Who? What? Where? When? Why? How big?
  • Stakeholders: List everyone affected (users, customer, etc.)
  • Needs: Functionality, ease of use, aesthetics
  • Constraints: Budget, materials, manufacturing, time, size
  • Ethics: Sustainability, safety, accessibility

Problem Statement: Good vs. Weak

Element Weak Example Strong Example
Problem "Cables are messy." "Our lab benches have three loose cables—power, USB, network—that create trip hazards and slow setup."
Requirements "Must hold cables." "Must hold up to 3 cables (≤6 mm Ø), allow insertion in ≤2 lb force, removal in ≤1 lb force."
Constraints "Use plastic." "Use PLA filament; cost ≤$1 per unit; fit in 120×120×120 mm print volume; complete by Week 3."

Key Tip: Be specific! Use numbers, measurements, and clear criteria.

DAY 2 Research Summary (Research)

Purpose:

Gather information to inform your solution

Research Activities:

  • Find 3–5 credible sources (websites, videos, products)
  • Search terms: "DIY cell phone stand", "Amazon cell phone stand", "phone holder designs"
  • Summarize existing solutions or components
  • Note features, materials, prices, pros/cons
  • Connect research to YOUR project constraints
  • Cite ALL sources (including AI if used!)

DAY 3 Brainstormed Ideas (Imagine)

Purpose:

Generate creative solutions with your team

Brainstorming Activities:

  • Team mind-mapping session (5+ ideas minimum)
  • For EACH idea: Write 2-3 sentences describing it
  • List pros and cons for each idea
  • Note how it meets constraints and ethics
  • Create paper/pencil sketches (clear labels!)
  • Photo-upload sketches to Google Doc
  • Document team roles in journals

Brainstorming Best Practices

DO

  • Go wild with ideas first!
  • Build on others' suggestions
  • "Yes, and..." not "No, but..."
  • Sketch quickly, iterate often
  • Consider different approaches
  • Think about user experience

DON'T

  • Judge ideas too quickly
  • Shoot down teammates' ideas
  • Worry about perfection
  • Limit yourself to "safe" ideas
  • Skip the sketching step
  • Design alone (it's teamwork!)

Remember: Quantity leads to quality. Generate lots of ideas THEN evaluate!

DAY 4 Evaluation (Pugh Matrix)

Purpose:

Choose the best idea using a decision matrix

Pugh Matrix Process:

  • Create table comparing all ideas to baseline (Idea 1)
  • Select 3–5 evaluation criteria (functionality, cost, ethics, etc.)
  • Assign weights to criteria (1–5, higher = more important)
  • Score each idea: + (better, +1), S (same, 0), - (worse, -1)
  • Multiply score × weight, sum for total
  • Justify top-scoring idea, tie to project goals

Pugh Matrix Example

Criteria (Weight) Idea 1 (Baseline) Idea 2 Idea 3
Cost (3) 0 + (3) S (0)
Durability (4) 0 S (0) - (-4)
Ease of Fabrication (2) 0 + (2) - (-2)
TOTAL SCORE 0 +5 -6

Result: Idea 2 scores highest (+5). It's cheaper and easier to make than baseline, with equal durability.
