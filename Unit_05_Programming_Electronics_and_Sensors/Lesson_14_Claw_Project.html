When to Use AI

The right tool for the right job.

⚡

Impact Driver

Fast. Powerful. Gets the job done in seconds.

But in the wrong situation — wrong fastener, too much torque — it strips the screw or cracks the material.

🔩

Screwdriver

Slow. Gives you feel and control.

You know exactly what's happening at every turn. Precision work done right.

AI is your impact driver — use it with judgment.

Reach for AI when…

  • You're stuck on a concept and need it explained differently
  • You have a plan and want starter code to annotate
  • You want to check your own reasoning against another source
  • You need to explore options quickly

Put it down when…

  • You haven't tried to understand it yourself first
  • You're copying output without reading it
  • The answer doesn't make sense and you accept it anyway
  • You couldn't explain the result to someone else

Ethics in AI Use

Integrity

There's a line between AI helping you learn and AI learning for you. Using AI to skip understanding isn't a shortcut — it's a trap. The quiz has no notes for a reason.

Honesty

Log every prompt you use. This isn't punishment — it's how engineers document their process. Hiding your sources doesn't make your work more impressive; it makes it less trustworthy.

Responsibility

If AI writes code that doesn't work, you still own the problem. In the real world, "AI told me so" is not an acceptable answer when a system fails. You are the engineer.

"AI-assisted" means you did the thinking. AI helped you do it faster.

The spec sheet and quiz together are how we verify that the work is yours.
If you own the understanding, both are easy. If you don't, both will show it.

AI in This Class

"If you can't explain it, you don't own it."

Rule 1 — Give Context

Tell AI who you are, what hardware you're using, and what you already know. Vague prompts give vague answers.

Rule 2 — Show Your Thinking

Share your plan first, then ask AI to help. AI improves your thinking better than it replaces it.

Rule 3 — Push Back

AI is wrong sometimes. If something doesn't make sense, say so. Accepting wrong answers is on you.

What AI use looks like here

  • Generate code — then annotate every line yourself
  • Explain concepts — then rewrite in your own words
  • Review your spec sheet — then verify what it changes
  • Log every prompt in your AI Log section

How you're graded

  • Spec sheet — accuracy & clarity, not polish
  • Live demo — code must work on real hardware
  • Quiz — everything in your spec sheet is fair game

The Challenge

Your claw must do this — automatically, every time:

📡 Serial trigger received
↓
🟢 Claw opens fully
↓ [place object]
⚙️ Claw closes slowly — feedback monitored each step
↓
🔵 Is feedback still changing?
YES → keep closing  |  NO → contact detected!
↓
💡 LED color = object size   (🟢 large   🔵 medium   🔴 small)
↓
🔍 Hold grip · monitor for slip · 🟡 flash if object moves

Why this is interesting

The claw can't see the object. It uses sensor feedback to know when it's touched something — the same principle used in industrial robot grippers.

What you'll build

  • Servo PWM control
  • Analog feedback / ADC
  • Feedback-controlled loops

Today: Assembly

First AI Prompt

Before you open the box, try this prompt:

"I am assembling a Pololu Micro Gripper Kit (product #3551). It uses a servo motor and a rack-and-pinion mechanism to move two parallel paddles. Give me step-by-step assembly instructions."

Then open the official Pololu guide and compare. Where did AI get it right? Where was it vague or wrong? Write this in your spec sheet.

Why this matters

AI doesn't have a camera in your kit. It may not know the exact order of assembly steps. Verifying AI output against a reliable source is an engineering skill — practice it now.

Assembly Checklist

  • Use the official Pololu assembly guide
  • Do not force any parts — the rack slides in one direction
  • The servo should be seated flush before securing
  • Four wires when done: green (pot feedback), white (servo signal), red (power), black (ground)
